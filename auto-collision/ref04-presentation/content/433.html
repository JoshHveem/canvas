Machine Finish Sanding
One way to match texture to adjacent OEM panels, and remove refinish defects, such as excessive orange peel and dirt nibs, is with a machine sander. Machine sanding for detailing uses a finishing sander like this instead of a regular dual-action sander. Finishing sanders have a shorter stroke then DA sanders. The shorter stroke and the use of an interface pad helps to control the aggressiveness of the sandscratches. First we’ll take initial film thickness readings to make sure there is enough film build to allow for sanding. To remove the defects on our panel we’ll start with dry P1500 grit paper attached to an interface pad. Before sanding on the panel, check to be sure the sander speed is not too fast. As we’re sanding, we make sure to move the sander back and forth, avoiding sanding in any one spot for too long, as this could remove too much clearcoat. The panel is wiped off to check progress, and see if all the defects are removed and texture is matched. Now we’ll switch to using a wet sanding P3000 grit disc and the interface pad. Wet the panel with a water spray bottle, and also apply water to the sanding disc. The sander is again moved in a back and forth motion on the panel making sure to keep both the panel and paper wet. A white slurry like this should be seen during the wet sanding process. After wiping the panel, we can see the wet sanding removed all the previous sancscratches, giving the panel a dull sheen. The area is again sanded with a 5000 grit wet sanding disc. This area is now ready for buffing and polishing.
